Alpha Driver
Jazz instruments punched far above their weight
Miles Davis and John Coltrane pieces held for just 4 years produced CAGRs of 50% and 36% respectively — outperforming even the trophy guitar lots on a risk-adjusted basis. Short post-2020 windows dramatically outperform the long-dated pre-2015 book.

Investment Signal
Performative provenance is the singular pricing variable
Every top-quartile CAGR lot was physically used at a culturally defining moment. Lots without a "touch" story — film scripts, personal jewellery, awards — underperform structurally, regardless of artist fame or cultural profile.

Concentration Risk
76.8% of portfolio value sits in a single sub-category
Guitars represent $52.2M of the $68M estimated value. While the category has delivered +205% total return, the illiquidity profile and insurance cost of this concentration is material. Instruments and lyrics diversify the return base but remain underweight.
76.8% Guitar weight by realized value

The Irsay collection demonstrates the asymmetric return profile of museum-grade provenance at scale. Instruments tied to defining cultural moments command scarcity premiums that compress correlation with public equities to near zero — but the data shows hold period structure matters equally: the 4–7 year bucket averaged 17.9% CAGR across 15 lots, against just 5.6% for the 8–12 year cohort. The 20+ year outliers partially recover due to extreme early undervaluation at acquisition.
